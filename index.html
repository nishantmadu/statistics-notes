The Core Idea
Why Do We Need Both?

In an ideal world, we'd collect data from every single person to get perfect answers. But in reality, that's almost always impossible — too expensive, too slow, or just impractical.

So instead, we take a smaller representative slice of the full group, study it carefully, and use those findings to make conclusions about everyone.

💡
Simple rule: Population = everyone. Sample = a carefully chosen few that represents everyone.
Island Example — from the Lecture
🏝️ Collecting Weights from 100,000 People
🌍 Population (N)
N = 100,000 people
All residents on the island

To collect everyone's weight you'd need to visit all 100K people — some might be away, some hard to reach. Practically impossible.

🔬 Sample (n)
n = 10,000 people
Randomly picked from the island

We pick 10K people, collect their weights, and use those results to draw conclusions about all 100K. Feasible, fast, and statistically valid.

Why not use full population?
Collecting data from everyone is expensive, slow, and often physically impossible. Some people are unreachable, some data changes over time, and it's unnecessary if a good sample represents the whole.
Why is a sample valid?
If chosen randomly and carefully, a sample mirrors the population's characteristics very closely. This is the entire foundation of inferential statistics — what we covered in L02.
Real-World Examples
🌍 Where Population vs Sample Shows Up in Real Life

📊
Customer Surveys

A company with 10M users doesn't survey everyone. They pick a sample of 5,000 → make product decisions for all 10M.

In Data Science
🤖 How This Applies to ML & DS
Your Dataset IS the Sample

When you train an ML model on a dataset (say 100K rows), that dataset is your sample. You're trying to build a model that works for the entire population — all future unseen data.

Train/Test Split = Sample Logic

When you split 80/20 for train-test — your test set is a sample used to infer how the model will perform on the full population of real-world data.

L06
Interview Question
🎯 One of the Most Asked Statistics Questions
💡
Why do we divide by n - 1 in sample variance instead of dividing by n?

This concept is called Bessel’s Correction.

📌 Sample Variance
📌 Unbiased Estimation
📌 Degree of Freedom
Formula Comparison
Population vs Sample Variance
🌍 Population Variance
σ² = Σ(X - μ)² / N

Uses complete population data.

🔬 Sample Variance
S² = Σ(X - x̄)² / (n - 1)

Uses sample data for estimating population variance.

📌
Population variance divides by N, while sample variance divides by n - 1.
Core Intuition
Why Does the Problem Happen?

When we use sample data, we are trying to estimate the behavior of the full population.

Population
Population contains all possible data points.
Sample
Sample contains only a small subset of the population.
⚠️
A sample may not perfectly represent the population every time.
Important Scenario
📊 What Happens if the Sample is Biased?

Suppose we randomly pick some points from a population.

Population
→
Pick Sample
→
Calculate Mean & Variance
Good Case
If the sample is well distributed, sample mean and variance become approximately equal to population mean and variance.
Bad Case
If we accidentally pick clustered points from only one side of the distribution, sample variance becomes much smaller than true population variance.
📉
Using division by n tends to underestimate the true population variance.
Main Reason
How n - 1 Fixes the Problem

Instead of dividing by n, we divide by a smaller number:

n - 1

Since:

n - 1 < n

The final variance value becomes slightly larger.

✅
This correction helps prevent underestimation of population variance.
Key Goal
Make sample variance a better and more unbiased estimate of the true population variance.

is called:

🎯
Bessel’s Correction

Degree of Freedom
What is DOF?
DOF = n - 1

DOF stands for:

📌 Degree of Freedom
📊
Degree of Freedom is heavily used in statistical tests like t-test and chi-square test.
ML & DS Perspective
🤖 Why This Matters in Data Science
Small Samples
In Data Science, we often work with limited sample data instead of full population data.
Model Generalization
Statistical corrections help ensure that our estimates generalize better to unseen population data.
💡
Bessel’s correction improves the reliability of variance estimation in real-world ML datasets.

What is Standard Deviation?

Variance tells us how spread out the data is.

But variance has one problem:

⚠️
Variance is in squared units, which is hard for humans to interpret.

Standard deviation fixes this by taking the square root of variance.

Standard Deviation = √Variance
🎯
Standard deviation tells us how far data points are from the mean.
Visual Intuition
📊 Understanding Distance from Mean
Simple Thinking
If mean is 3:
• 4 is 1 standard deviation away from the mean
• 5 is 2 standard deviations away from the mean
• 2 is 1 standard deviation to the left

Population Standard Deviation
σ = √σ²
σ² = Σ(Xi - μ)² / N
📌 σ → Population Std Dev
📌 μ → Population Mean
📌 N → Population Size

Sample Standard Deviation
S = √S²
S² = Σ(Xi - x̄)² / (n - 1)
📌 S → Sample Std Dev
📌 x̄ → Sample Mean
📌 n-1 → Bessel Correction

Core Understanding
A Random Variable Converts An Outcome Into A Value

A random variable is a function whose values come from a random process or experiment.

It is usually written using a capital letter such as X. The experiment produces an outcome, and the random variable assigns a value to that outcome.

💡
Simple meaning: a random variable is a rule that turns uncertain real-world outcomes into numbers we can analyze.
Notation
Common Symbol
Xrandom variable
xone observed value
Outcomeresult of experiment

Example: if X represents the result of tossing a coin, then one observed value may be 0 or 1.

Big Picture
From Experiment To Random Variable

The x-axis below shows possible outcomes from an experiment. The y-axis shows the assigned numerical value used for analysis.

Dataset Example
Coin Toss Dataset

Suppose we toss a coin multiple times. The raw outcome is text, but the random variable maps each outcome to a number.

Raw outcomes:
Head, Tail, Tail, Head, Tail

Random variable rule:
Head → 0, Tail → 1

Numerical dataset:
X = [0, 1, 1, 0, 1]

Now the coin-toss experiment has become numerical data that can be used for probability, PMF, Bernoulli distribution and modeling.

Intuition
Why Not Just Say Variable?

A normal variable can simply store a known value. A random variable gets its value from an uncertain process.

experiment
→
outcome
→
number

That final number is the value taken by the random variable.

When k = 1, the formula becomes p. When k = 0, the formula becomes 1 − p.

1 = Uses smartphone A
0 = Does not use smartphone A

Data: 1, 1, 0, 1, 0, 1, 1, 0, 1, 0

Here, 6 out of 10 customers use it, so p = 0.60 and q = 0.40.

Mean
Expected Value

The mean of a Bernoulli distribution is:

E(X) = 0·q + 1·p = p

For the smartphone example, E(X) = 0.60. In the long run, about 60% of customers are expected to use smartphone A.

Where Is The Center?
Median0 if p < 0.5, 1 if p > 0.5
Mode1 if p > q, 0 if q > p
If p = qBoth 0 and 1 are equally likely

In our example, p = 0.60, so the mode is 1.

Variance
How Much Does It Vary?

The variance and standard deviation are:

Var(X) = pq
σ = √(pq)

For p = 0.60 and q = 0.40, Var(X) = 0.24 and σ ≈ 0.49.

The binomial distribution models the number of successes in a fixed number of independent trials, where every trial has only two outcomes: success or failure.

Parameters And Support
X ~ B(n,p)Binomial random variable
nNumber of trials
pProbability of success on each trial
q1 − p, probability of failure
kNumber of successes, from 0 to n
PMF
Probability Mass Function

For a binomial random variable, the probability of exactly k successes in n trials is:

P(X = k) = C(n,k) pk(1 − p)n−k,   k = 0,1,2,...,n
C(n,k) = n! / [k!(n−k)!]

The C(n,k) term counts how many different orders can produce the same number of successes.

Real Dataset Example
Bank Visitors Per Hour

Suppose we observe how many people visit a bank at each hourly interval:

12 PM → 1 visitor
1 PM → 2 visitors
2 PM → 3 visitors
3 PM → 3 visitors
4 PM → 4 visitors

Each value is a count of events in a fixed one-hour interval.

λexpected average number of events per interval
xactual event count we are asking about
x values0, 1, 2, 3, ...

PMF Formula
How The Probability Is Computed

Because Poisson is discrete, we use a probability mass function.

P(X = x) = e−λ λx / x!
where λ = average rate and x = event count

Example question: if λ = 3 visitors per hour, what is the probability of exactly 5 visitors in an hour?

P(X = 5) = e−3 × 35 / 5!
P(X = 5) ≈ 0.101
So the probability is about 10.1%.

Std. deviation√λ

Combining Poisson Probabilities

If the question asks for exactly 4 or exactly 5 events, compute each probability and add them.

P(X = 4 or X = 5) = P(X = 4) + P(X = 5)

If the question says “less than or equal to 3,” add every probability from 0 to 3.

P(X ≤ 3) = P(X=0) + P(X=1) + P(X=2) + P(X=3)

Simple question
How many standard deviations is 4.25 away from the mean when μ = 4 and σ = 1?

The answer is 0.25 standard deviations above the mean.

Formula
The Z-Score Formula
z = (xᵢ − μ) / σ
xᵢ = individual value, μ = mean, σ = standard deviation

If z is positive, the value is above the mean. If z is negative, the value is below the mean.

Original X → 1, 2, 3, 4, 5
Mean μ → 3
Assume σ → 1 for simple calculation

Std. deviation1
Variance1

The shape stays bell-like, but the scale changes from original units to standard-deviation units.

Assume a Normal distribution with μ = 4 and σ = 1. For x = 4.25:

z = (4.25 − 4) / 1 = 0.25

This means 4.25 is 0.25 standard deviations to the right of the mean.

When Z Is Negative

z = (2.5 − 4) / 1 = −1.5

This means 2.5 is 1.5 standard deviations to the left of the mean.

Core Understanding
What Is Bayes Theorem?

Bayes Theorem is a way to update probability when new information is given.

Instead of asking only “What is the probability of A?”, Bayes theorem helps us ask:

What is the probability of A, given that B has already happened?
🧠
Simple idea: Bayes theorem is probability after seeing evidence.
Why It Matters
Bayesian Thinking

Bayesian statistics is an approach to data analysis and parameter estimation based on Bayes theorem.

It is useful when we want to revise our belief after observing new data.

Before evidenceprior probability
After evidenceupdated probability
Big Picture
From Conditional Probability To Bayes Theorem

Bayes theorem helps us update a probability after new information is known. The figure below shows how conditional probability leads to the final Bayes theorem formula step by step.

Independent Events
Events That Do Not Affect Each Other

If events are independent, one event does not change the probability of another event.

Example
Rolling a dice: P(1) = 1/6, P(2) = 1/6, and so on. One outcome does not change another future roll.
Coin toss
P(Head) = 0.5 and P(Tail) = 0.5. If one toss gives Head, the next toss is still not affected.
Dependent Events
Events That Affect Each Other

Dependent events are different. Once one event happens, it changes the probability of the next event.

Bag of marbles:
3 yellow marbles · 2 red marbles · total = 5

If we first take a red marble, then the bag now has only 4 marbles left. So the probability of getting yellow changes.

P(Red first) = 2 / 5 P(Yellow after Red) = 3 / 4

Formula
Bayes Theorem Formula
P(A | B) = [ P(A) × P(B | A) ] / P(B)
P(A | B)probability of A given B is true

P(A)independent probability of A

How To Read It

Bayes theorem says:

Updated probability = old probability × evidence support / total evidence probability

So we are not blindly guessing. We update the probability after observing evidence.

Confidence Interval And Margin Of Error
Confidence Interval · Point Estimate · Margin Of Error · Z Table · Lower And Upper Bound
Core Understanding
Why Confidence Interval Is Needed

A point estimate gives only one number. For example, if a sample mean is x̄ = 520, that is our best single guess for the population mean.

But one sample may be slightly above or below the true population mean. So instead of trusting only one number, we create a range.

💡
Simple meaning: A confidence interval says, “Based on my sample, the true average is likely somewhere between this lower value and this upper value.”
Mental Model
Point Estimate vs Interval Estimate

Think of a point estimate like throwing one dart at the target. A confidence interval draws a safe zone around that dart because the dart may not land exactly at the center.

Confidence Interval = Point Estimate ± Margin Of Error

Here, the margin of error is the extra room we add on both sides of the sample mean.

Big Picture
From One Sample Mean To A Confidence Range

The figure shows how a single sample mean becomes a lower and upper confidence interval using margin of error.

Formula
Confidence Interval Formula

The general formula is:

Confidence Interval = Point Estimate ± Margin Of Error

For a sample mean, the point estimate is usually the sample mean x̄.

Confidence Interval = x̄ ± Margin Of Error
Margin Of Error
Z-test and t-test versions

If population standard deviation σ is known, use the z-based margin of error:

Margin Of Error = zα/2 × (σ / √n)

If population standard deviation is unknown and we use sample standard deviation s, use the t-based version:

Margin Of Error = tα/2, df × (s / √n)

For a 95% confidence interval:

Area to the left = 1 − 0.025 = 0.9750

The margin of error is 39.2. This means we add and subtract 39.2 from the sample mean.

Layman Interpretation
What Does 95% Confident Mean?

It does not mean there is a 95% chance that this exact interval contains the population mean. The population mean is fixed; we just do not know it.

In simple terms, it means: if we repeatedly took many samples and built intervals the same way, about 95% of those intervals would capture the true population mean.

🧠
Memory trick: Confidence interval is a range created from sample data to estimate where the true population average may lie.
Common Confusion
Confidence Interval vs Hypothesis Testing

In hypothesis testing, we use confidence regions and rejection regions to decide whether to reject H₀.

In confidence intervals, we calculate the actual number range around the sample estimate.

Hypothesis testing asks: Should we reject H₀? Confidence interval asks: What range is likely for the population mean?

What Does Chi-square Test Do?

A Chi-square test is used when we are working with categorical data. Categorical data means values are groups or labels, not continuous numbers.

Examples of categorical data are bike color, left-handed/right-handed, male/female, pass/fail, or product category.

💡

Mental Model
Theory vs Reality

Think of the Chi-square test like checking whether a claim about groups is believable.

Theory says: category counts should look like this. Sample data says: category counts actually look like this. Chi-square test asks: Are these two close enough, or are they too different?

If the observed counts are very different from the expected counts, the theory may not fit the sample well.

“Goodness of fit” asks whether the sample data fits a theory.

Theory: 1/3 people like Yellow, 1/3 like Red, 1/3 like Orange Observed sample: Yellow = 22, Red = 17, Orange = 59 Question: Does the observed sample support the theory?

If the sample looks close to the theory, it is a good fit. If it is very different, it may be a poor fit.

A Chi-square goodness of fit test checks whether the category counts we observe in a sample are close to what an older theory, population distribution, or expectation says.

In 2010, a city census said people were distributed by weight as: 20% less than 50 kg, 30% between 50 and 75 kg, and 50% greater than 75 kg. In 2020, we sampled 500 people and observed counts of 140, 160, and 200. At α = 0.05, did the population weight distribution change?
Simple Intuition
Old Pattern vs New Sample

Think of the 2010 percentages as the expected pattern. The 2020 sample counts are the observed pattern.

Expected = what should happen if the old 2010 pattern is still true Observed = what we actually saw in the 2020 sample Chi-square asks: Are observed counts too different from expected counts?

Step 2
Write The Hypotheses

The null hypothesis usually says the observed data matches the expected distribution.

H₀: The 2020 weight distribution meets the 2010 expectation. H₁: The 2020 weight distribution does not meet the 2010 expectation.

In simple words: H₀ says “no real change.” H₁ says “there is a change.”

df = number of categories − 1 df = 3 − 1 = 2

In simple words: the population weight pattern appears to have changed over the last ten years.

Core Understanding
What Is ANOVA?

ANOVA stands for Analysis of Variance. It is a statistical method used to compare the means of two or more groups.

ANOVA asks: Are the group averages really different, or do they look different only because of random sample variation?

In simple words, ANOVA helps us check whether multiple groups have meaningfully different average values.

Why Not Only Z-test Or T-test?
When Groups Become More Than Two

Z-tests and t-tests are commonly used when comparing averages in simpler cases. But when we have three or more groups, doing many separate t-tests can become messy.

Example
If we want to compare average marks of students taught by Method A, Method B, and Method C, ANOVA gives one clean test for all groups together.
Big Picture
ANOVA Compares Group Means

The idea is simple: collect data from multiple groups, calculate each group mean, and then test whether the differences between the group means are large enough to matter.

Component 1
What Is A Factor?

A factor is the main category or condition we are studying.

Medicine example
The factor is medicine dosage, because we want to see whether different dosage groups have different average results.
Payment example
The factor is mode of payment, because we may want to compare average transaction amount across payment methods.
Component 2
What Are Levels?

Levels are the specific groups inside a factor.

Factor: Medicine dosage Levels: 5 mg, 10 mg, 15 mg Factor: Mode of payment Levels: Google Pay, PhonePe, IMPS, NEFT

So, a factor is the broad variable, and levels are the groups inside that variable.

What ANOVA Is Really Checking

ANOVA compares two kinds of variation:

1. Variation between groups Are the group averages far apart? 2. Variation within groups Are values inside each group naturally scattered?

If the group means are far apart compared to the normal variation inside groups, ANOVA suggests that at least one group mean is different.

Hypothesis Idea
How The Test Is Framed

For ANOVA, the null hypothesis usually says all group means are equal.

H₀: all group means are equal H₁: at least one group mean is different

Important: ANOVA tells us whether a difference exists, but not exactly which group is different. For that, we later use post-hoc tests.

Core Understanding
Why Do ANOVA Assumptions Matter?

ANOVA is used to compare the average of two or more groups. But before using ANOVA, we should check whether the data roughly satisfies a few assumptions.

Think of assumptions like basic conditions. If these conditions are badly broken, the ANOVA result may become misleading.

💡
Simple idea: ANOVA can compare group means only when the groups are collected properly and their data behaves in a reasonably fair way.
Big Picture
Four Main Assumptions Of ANOVA

The figure below shows the four checks we should think about before applying ANOVA.

Assumption 1
Normality Of Sampling Distribution Of Means

This means the sample means should roughly follow a normal distribution.

In simple words: if we repeatedly take samples and calculate their averages, those averages should form a bell-shaped pattern.

🔔
Memory trick: Whenever you hear “sampling distribution of mean,” think about the Central Limit Theorem.
Assumption: sample means are approximately normally distributed
Assumption 2
Absence Of Outliers

Outliers are extremely high or extremely low values that can pull the group average too much.

ANOVA compares means, so if outliers are present, the means may no longer represent the groups properly.

Example:
Group marks: 50, 52, 55, 54, 98

The value 98 may be an outlier. We should investigate it before applying ANOVA.

Assumption 3
Homogeneity Of Variance

This means the groups should have similar variance, or similar spread.

In simple words: one group should not be very tightly packed while another group is extremely scattered.

σ₁² ≈ σ₂² ≈ σ₃²

If we compare three medicine dosage groups, the variation inside each dosage group should be roughly similar.

Assumption 4
Independent And Random Samples

The samples should be selected randomly, and one observation should not influence another observation.

For example, if we are comparing three teaching methods, one student's marks should not be copied from another student's marks.

🎯
Simple meaning: each row in the dataset should be collected fairly and independently.

Core Understanding
What Does “Partitioning Of Variance” Mean?

ANOVA compares the means of two or more groups, but it does that by looking at variance.

Instead of only asking “are the group averages different?”, ANOVA asks a deeper question:

Are the differences between group means big compared to the natural variation inside each group?
💡
Simple idea: ANOVA splits total variation into two parts: variation between groups and variation within groups.
Big Picture
Between-group vs Within-group Variance

The F test compares two types of variance. If the between-group variance is much larger than the within-group variance, then the group means are probably not all the same.

We want to know:

ANOVA starts by assuming all group means are equal.

We do not write that every mean is different. The alternate hypothesis only says at least one mean is different.

The F distribution is right-skewed. We reject H₀ only if the calculated F value falls in the right rejection region.

If calculated F > 3.5546 → reject H₀ If calculated F ≤ 3.5546 → fail to reject H₀

Step 6A
Sum Of Squares Between Groups

SS between measures how much the group totals/means differ from each other.

SS between = Σ(Aᵢ² / n) − T² / N = (57² / 7 + 47² / 7 + 21² / 7) − 125² / 21 = 842.71 − 744.05 = 98.67

Simple meaning: this captures the variation due to different dosage groups.

Step 6B
Sum Of Squares Within Groups

SS within measures how much values vary inside each dosage group.

ΣY² = 9² + 8² + 7² + ... + 2² = 853 SS within = ΣY² − Σ(Aᵢ² / n) = 853 − 842.71 = 10.29

Simple meaning: this captures natural variation among people inside the same dosage group.

What Does This Mean?

We reject the idea that all three dosage means are equal.

At least one dosage group has a different average headache relief rating.

In simple words, the medication dosage seems to make a difference in headache relief ratings.

ANOVA tells us that there is a difference somewhere, but it does not directly tell us exactly which groups are different. For that, we usually do a post-hoc test later.
